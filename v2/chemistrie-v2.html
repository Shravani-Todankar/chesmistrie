— Manifesto · 1,140 words —

We did not set out to start a brand.
We set out to keep a promise.

A decade behind the same compounding bench in Houston, weighing actives by the milligram for the women in our families who had been failed — politely, repeatedly — by everything on the shelf. Wives wanted retinol that didn't burn. Mothers wanted vitamin C that didn't oxidise in a week. Best friends wanted a moisturiser they could hand down.

So we began, quietly, in concentrations the industry deemed too expensive to mass-produce. CHEMISTRIE is what happened when our patient list became a waiting list, and our waiting list outgrew the lab.

Twelve formulas. Hand-numbered. Compounded by pharmacists. Tested on the women we love before it is ever offered to you.

— Page 24 · Anatomy —

A bottle, dissected.

Eight numbered annotations on the Renewal Serum N°01. What it is. What it isn't. Why every milligram landed where it did.

01.

Brass weighted cap

14 g of artisan-finished brass. Designed to sit upright on a marble counter for a decade.

02.

Nitrogen-sealed neck

Filled under nitrogen and crimped within ninety seconds. The active never meets oxygen.

03.

Letterpress label

One press at a time, on cotton card. The italic No. 01 is hand-set, not printed.

04.

Pharmacist countersign

Initials of the compounding pharmacist on duty. Filed against your batch number in the lab log.

05.

UV-blocking glass, 4mm

A double-walled forest tint. Cuts visible light to 0.4% — actives stay potent for the full year.

06.

Retinaldehyde at 0.05%

Eleven times faster than retinol. Half the irritation. The dose only a pharmacist would risk.

07.

Niacinamide at 4%

The barrier insurance behind the retinaldehyde. Quiet, daily, undramatic improvement.

08.

Hand-blown base, Houston

The slight base ripple is the glassblower's signature. No two bottles in a batch are identical.

— Page 64 · Interview

A conversation
with the bench.

— Why pharmacists, why now?

"Because the industry sold us serums but never the dose. We weighed it ourselves until our family no longer needed to ask which one to use. CHEMISTRIE is just that — the dose, written down."

— What did you refuse to compromise?

"Concentration. The whole industry is built on whispering '0.05%' on the back of a bottle and trusting nobody reads the small print. We chose to start with the small print, then design the bottle around it."

— And the women you love?

"They are the standard. If a formula does not make her say, on the second night, 'something has changed,' it does not leave the bench."
